## What You'll See

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to give CoWork a structured document brief, iterate on specific sections without losing the whole document, import existing documents for editing, and export your output in a usable format.

## Why This Matters

The difference between Claude CoWork and a chatbot for document work is that CoWork maintains the whole document as a working artifact, not a chat response. You can iterate on Section 3 without rewriting Sections 1 and 2. You can import a draft you wrote last month and ask CoWork to restructure it. This changes what is possible — and how long it takes.

## Core Concepts

### Giving CoWork a Document Brief (Not a Prompt)

When you ask Claude.ai to write a report, you get one response — and then you start over if you want changes. CoWork works differently. You give it a **brief**: a structured description of what you need, who it is for, and what it should accomplish.

A good document brief answers four questions:

1. **What is this document?** (Type: proposal, SOP, report, analysis)
2. **Who is the audience?** (Board, customers, new employees, your team)
3. **What is the desired outcome?** (Approve budget, understand process, make a decision)
4. **What should it cover?** (Key topics, data points to include, sections required)

**Example brief:**
> "Create a 4-page executive proposal for our board requesting budget approval for a new customer support tooling platform. Audience: non-technical board members. Outcome: approve $85,000 budget. Sections needed: problem statement, proposed solution, cost breakdown, ROI projection, implementation timeline. Tone: professional and data-driven."

This brief produces a structured, audience-appropriate document on the first pass — not a draft you have to reshape from scratch.

### Iterating on Sections

Once CoWork has produced a document, you can ask it to revise specific sections without touching the rest:

> "Rewrite the problem statement to be more urgent — it reads too neutral. Keep the rest unchanged."

> "Expand the ROI projection section. Add a 3-year model showing cumulative savings. Keep the rest of the document as-is."

> "The implementation timeline is too optimistic. Revise it to assume a 3-month procurement delay."

CoWork treats the document as a persistent artifact. Each revision builds on the previous version rather than starting fresh.

<TipBlock>Name each section clearly in your document. When you say "revise the problem statement," CoWork needs to find that section. Clear section headers (using ## in Markdown) make iteration much more reliable.</TipBlock>

### Importing Existing Documents

You can give CoWork a document you have already written and ask it to improve, restructure, or expand it:

**In the browser interface:** Upload the file using the attachment button. Supported formats: PDF, Word (.docx), Markdown (.md), plain text (.txt).

**In the desktop client:** Use the file browser to select documents directly from your computer.

Once imported, you can ask:
> "Restructure this proposal to lead with the ROI section instead of the problem statement. Keep all content."

> "This SOP is 12 pages. Condense it to 4 pages while keeping all critical steps."

> "Improve the writing quality throughout — make it more direct and remove filler phrases."

### Exporting Outputs

CoWork can export documents in several formats:

| Format | Best for |
|--------|---------|
| Markdown (.md) | Portability, version control, developer tools |
| Word (.docx) | Microsoft Office users, clients who need .docx |
| PDF | Final delivery, sharing with stakeholders |
| Plain text (.txt) | Pasting into other tools |

To export, look for the download or export button in the CoWork interface. If exporting is not available, copy the content manually — Markdown copies cleanly into most editors.

### Common Document Tasks and How to Brief Them

**Standard Operating Procedure (SOP):**
> "Write an SOP for onboarding a new account manager. Audience: HR and the new hire. Format: numbered steps with substeps. Include: pre-arrival checklist, day 1 schedule, first-week milestones, 30/60/90 day goals."

**Project proposal:**
> "Write a project proposal requesting approval to migrate our data warehouse from on-premise to Snowflake. Decision-makers: CTO and CFO. Include: current pain points, proposed solution, costs (one-time + recurring), risks, timeline."

**Meeting summary:**
> "Summarize the following meeting notes into an executive summary for stakeholders who were not present. Format: 3-sentence overview, decisions made, action items with owners."

### The Review-Before-Sending Rule

Every document CoWork produces should be reviewed by a human before it goes anywhere. CoWork can produce plausible-sounding but factually incorrect content — especially for numbers, names, and specific details.

After CoWork produces a draft:
1. Read the full document once for sense and tone
2. Verify any numbers, dates, or named facts
3. Check that the document matches the brief (right sections, right length, right audience)
4. Make any final edits manually before sending

Do not send a CoWork document directly without review. The review step is your professional quality check.

## Key Takeaways

- Give CoWork a structured brief (what, who, outcome, sections) rather than a vague prompt.
- CoWork maintains the document as an artifact — iterate on sections without rewriting everything.
- Import existing documents for restructuring, condensing, or quality improvements.
- Export to Markdown, Word, or PDF depending on your workflow.
- Always review CoWork documents before sending — verify all facts, numbers, and names.
