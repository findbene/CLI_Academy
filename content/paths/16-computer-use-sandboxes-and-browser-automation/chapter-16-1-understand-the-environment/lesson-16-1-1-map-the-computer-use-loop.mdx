# Map the computer-use loop

## Objective
By the end of this lesson, you will be able to describe the fundamental "computer-use loop" and create a simple diagram representing its steps: observe, act, observe, and verify. This understanding will be a crucial foundation for all your interactions with the Command Line Interface (CLI).

## Walkthrough

Welcome! Let's explore the fundamental "computer-use loop." Whether you're clicking icons or typing commands in the CLI, you're always following this simple, repeating cycle. Understanding it will build your confidence and make learning new tools much easier.

Here are the steps:

1.  **Observe:** You look at the computer. What do you see? What information is displayed? What problem needs solving, or what task do you want to accomplish? *Example: You see a document icon and you want to open it.*
2.  **Act:** Based on your observation, you take action. This might be clicking, typing a command, or selecting an option. In future lessons, our "acts" will be carefully chosen, safe commands. *Example: You double-click the document icon.*
3.  **Observe (Again!):** After your action, you immediately look at the computer again. Did anything change? Did the document open? Did an error message appear? What is the *new* state of things? *Example: The document's content is now visible on screen, or a pop-up says "file not found."*
4.  **Verify:** This is the critical step to confirm: Did your action achieve *exactly* what you intended? Is the task truly complete and correct? If not, you loop back to the first "Observe" to figure out why and try another "Act." *Example: You see the correct document open, and you're ready to start working. Success!*

**Your Deliverable:**
Please create a simple diagram or textual representation of this four-step loop. The goal is to show the flow from one step to the next, representing the continuous cycle.

Here's an example of a simple textual representation you could provide:
`Observe --> Act --> Observe --> Verify`
(Feel free to add an arrow from `Verify` back to `Observe` to show it's a continuous loop!)
