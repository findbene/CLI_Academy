# Choose a safe first deployment shape

## Objective

Hello there! Making your very first deployment is an exciting step, and our top priority is making it a safe and comfortable experience for you. In this lesson, you'll explore the most common places, or "shapes," where you can run your Claw application. You'll learn the pros and cons of deploying on your local machine, a Virtual Private Server (VPS), or a dedicated physical device, especially from a beginner's safety and learning perspective.

By the end of this lesson, you will be able to choose the safest and most suitable first deployment shape for your Claw application based on your current device setup and experience level, and clearly explain why you made that choice.

## Walkthrough

Let's explore your options for where Claw can live. Thinking about this choice carefully helps ensure a smooth, safe learning experience.

1.  **Consider Your Local Machine**
    Your local machine is the computer you're using right now.
    *   **What it means:** Running Claw directly on your personal laptop or desktop.
    *   **Safety & Beginner-Friendly Aspects:**
        *   **Pros:** It's the easiest to access – no extra setup or cost. You're already familiar with it. For a first deployment, this is often the most straightforward way to learn and experiment without introducing new complex infrastructure.
        *   **Cons:** While we'll guide you safely, a small risk exists of potentially impacting other applications on your daily-use machine if not contained properly. It also uses your machine's resources.
    *   **Best for:** Absolute beginners who want to get started immediately, focus purely on Claw, and have full, physical control over their environment.

2.  **Explore a Virtual Private Server (VPS)**
    A VPS is like renting a virtual computer over the internet.
    *   **What it means:** A remote server provided by a hosting company, which you access via the command line (usually SSH).
    *   **Safety & Beginner-Friendly Aspects:**
        *   **Pros:** It's completely isolated from your personal computer. If Claw misbehaves, it won't affect your machine. It's also always online.
        *   **Cons:** This option costs money, requires more technical setup (like using SSH and basic Linux commands), and introduces networking concepts that can add complexity for a first-timer. Debugging can also be trickier remotely.
    *   **Best for:** Beginners who are already comfortable with basic CLI Linux commands and want a truly isolated environment from the start, and are okay with a slightly steeper learning curve.

3.  **Think About a Dedicated Physical Device**
    This means using a separate piece of hardware specifically for Claw.
    *   **What it means:** Deploying Claw on an old laptop, a Raspberry Pi, or any other spare computer you might have.
    *   **Safety & Beginner-Friendly Aspects:**
        *   **Pros:** Offers complete isolation from your daily machine, similar to a VPS but with physical control. You can experiment freely without any risk to your main computer.
        *   **Cons:** Requires purchasing or sourcing extra hardware, which adds cost and physical setup time. It also needs its own operating system installation and maintenance.
    *   **Best for:** Those who have spare hardware available and prioritize maximum physical isolation and control, and are comfortable with hardware setup.
