## What You'll See

By the end of this lesson, you will have the core prompting framework for CoWork, understand what makes a task well-specified versus vague, know when to redirect versus restart, and recognize the "draft and review" mindset that produces better results than expecting perfect first outputs.

## Why This Matters

The difference between a useful CoWork output and a frustrating one is almost always in how the task was given. CoWork does not read minds. It executes on what you specify — and fills gaps with plausible-sounding content when you leave them open. This lesson gives you the framework to eliminate those gaps.

## Core Concepts

### Why CoWork Needs Outcome-Oriented Prompts

Chatbots respond to open-ended questions because they are designed for conversation. CoWork is an agent — it executes tasks. When you give it an open-ended question, it has to invent the parameters (Who is this for? How long? What format? What decision does this support?) rather than being told them.

Invented parameters produce inconsistent outputs. Specified parameters produce reliable ones.

### The OGRR Framework

Every significant CoWork task should answer four questions:

**O — Objective:** What is this output for? What decision or action does it support?

**G — Given (context):** What do I already know? What documents, data, or constraints are relevant?

**R — Required format:** What should the output look like? Length, structure, sections, tone.

**R — Review criteria:** How will I evaluate this output? What makes it good or bad?

**Example (weak prompt):**
> "Write a competitor analysis."

**Example (OGRR prompt):**
> "**O:** Our VP of Sales needs a 2-page briefing before a call with a prospect who just came from our competitor. Goal: help the rep handle objections about our product gaps.  
> **G:** Our main competitor is [Company X]. Their recent pricing page and G2 reviews are attached.  
> **R:** Format: 2 pages max. Sections: what we do better, what they do better (honest), best objection responses for each gap.  
> **R:** Good output = the rep could use this before a call without any editing."

The OGRR prompt produces a usable first draft. The weak prompt produces a generic article.

### Breaking Large Tasks Into CoWork-Sized Chunks

CoWork works better on focused tasks than sprawling ones. If your output needs to be 20 pages, do not ask for it all at once.

**Instead:**
1. Ask for the outline first (approve or adjust it)
2. Ask CoWork to write Section 1 with specific parameters
3. Review Section 1, then continue to Section 2
4. Compile the full document at the end

This workflow gives you quality control at each step and prevents CoWork from going far in the wrong direction.

### How Much Context to Give

More relevant context produces better outputs. The context does not need to be long — it needs to be specific.

**Context that helps:**
- Your role and your audience's role
- The decision the output supports
- Constraints (budget, timeline, word limit, audience knowledge level)
- Examples of good output ("similar to a McKinsey executive brief")
- Existing documents to reference or build on

**Context that does not help:**
- Long explanations of company history unrelated to the task
- Instructions about what not to include (specify what to include instead)
- Vague quality descriptors ("make it really good")

### When CoWork Goes Off-Track

CoWork will sometimes produce output that misses the mark — wrong tone, wrong structure, wrong focus. There are two ways to handle this:

**Redirect (when the structure is mostly right):**
> "The outline is good but the tone is too casual. Rewrite it for a board audience — more formal, data-forward, less narrative."

> "The first section is too long. Condense it to 3 bullet points. Keep the rest as-is."

Redirecting works when CoWork understood the task but got a parameter wrong.

**Restart (when the direction is fundamentally wrong):**
If CoWork produced something that misunderstands the fundamental goal, it is often faster to start over with a better brief than to patch the output through redirects.

> "Let's start over. Here's a cleaner brief: [revised OGRR]"

The signal to restart: you have made 3+ redirect requests and the output is still not right.

### The Draft-and-Review Mental Model

The most common mistake professionals make with CoWork: expecting it to produce a final output on the first try.

CoWork produces **drafts**. Your job is to review and refine them. This is not a failure of the tool — it is how knowledge work with AI actually operates.

The right mental model:
- **First pass:** CoWork produces a structured draft in 30 seconds
- **Your review:** 5-10 minutes to read, verify facts, adjust tone, correct structure
- **Second pass (optional):** CoWork implements your revisions
- **Final:** You make the last editorial decisions

This is still dramatically faster than writing from scratch. The professionals who get the most value from CoWork are the ones who expect a fast first draft to refine, not a perfect final product to send.

<TipBlock>Set up a simple checklist for reviewing CoWork outputs: (1) Does it answer the actual question? (2) Are facts/numbers accurate? (3) Is the tone right for the audience? (4) Would I be comfortable putting my name on this? Four questions, two minutes.</TipBlock>

### Using CoWork to Accelerate Your Thinking

One of the most underrated uses of CoWork: give it your half-formed idea and ask it to structure it.

> "I have a vague sense that we should restructure our pricing. Here's what I'm thinking: [paragraph of rough thoughts]. Help me organize this into a logical argument I could present to my CFO."

CoWork does not replace your thinking — it helps you externalize and structure it faster. You bring the knowledge and judgment; CoWork provides the structure and language.

## Key Takeaways

- Use the OGRR framework: Objective, Given, Required format, Review criteria.
- Break large outputs into chunks — outline first, then section by section.
- Specific context (role, audience, decision, constraints) produces better outputs than vague context.
- Redirect when the structure is mostly right; restart when the direction is wrong.
- Expect drafts to refine, not finals to send. CoWork accelerates your process, not replaces it.
